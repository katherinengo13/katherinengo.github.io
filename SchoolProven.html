How might we improve how college students research their classes when they are planning out their schedules?

Background

As a college student myself, who spends some time figuring out which classes to take every quarter, I decided I wanted to learn more about how other people figure out which professor they should take for the quarter so that I can improve the experience. I then decided to go through the design process and create an app with two other UCSD students in order to create a community/review site where students can research their classes with confidence!

Needfinding

In order to learn more about how students choose their classes, I decided to conduct interviews so that I can determine what are the specific needs students have when researching for their classes. I asked 3 individuals (1 alumni and 2 undergraduate students) to walk me through their process of how they choose their classes. I had them open up the websites they use and show me how they use them and why. I also asked them a few questions about reliability and trust when it came to using different sources to gather information about their classes.

"I do research so that I don't take a class in the dark."

Takeaways

1) Students usually use ratemyprofessor, *CAPE, and their friend’s opinions.

2) 3/3 said that their top reliable source is their friends because they find them more reliable and trustworthy since they know they personally.

3) Students search through multiple sources in order to find answers to their questions about the class.

4) Individuals are sometimes unsure if reviews are trustworthy

*CAPE - Course and Professor Evaluations

Personas

Laura

Laura is a third-year college student who has finished all her general education requirements. She is looking for classes that are required for her major and wants to find good professors that will help her understand the material. She wants to know about specific aspects of the classes, like the grading, workload, and difficulty.

Joseph

Joseph is a first-year college student who is simply looking for general education classes he can take. He wants to take relatively easy classes so that he can have a smooth transition into college. He is aiming to take a diverse set of classes so that he find out what he likes.

Problem Statement:

Students need to open many resources to decide which classes to take and when students use review websites, there is a lack of verification and trust. Students read reviews but are often unsure if the review is trustworthy or relevant to them as a student.

Solution:

A review app that encourages more interactions between students utilizing the reviews and students writing the reviews. With reviewer profiles that provide a reviewer’s year, major, and classes, users can conclude if a review is trustworthy or relevant.

Wireframes & Prototypes

Paper Prototypes

We created paper prototypes of the main functions we wanted to focus on, which were the reviewer profile and Q&A interactions so that we can receive feedback on the general flow and functionality of the app before proceeding with higher fidelity prototypes.

Changes we made based on feedback

1) Add a breakdown of how many people gave certain review ratings (ex. 10 people gave 5 stars, 6 people gave 4 stars, etc.)

Our evaluators wanted to see the breakdown so that they could get the whole picture since an average rating might not be enough

2) Keep locations of back buttons consistent

Having the back buttons in a consistent location would make the flows more efficient

3) Reduce the number of pop-ups

Having multiple layers of popups could overwhelm the user and increase friction in the flows

Reviewer Profiles

According to the needfinding takeaways, students tend to trust their friends’ opinions the most when deciding the classes. This is due to the personal connection they have with their friends and the fact that they can ask their friends any question about the class or professor. So for the app, we decided to create reviewer profiles. Students are still able to write reviews anonymously, however, if they create an account then they can input some information about their student profile so that other students can see their major, year, and other classes they took. Names are still completely anonymous.

By incorporating reviewer profiles into the app, students are able to determine if a review is trustworthy or relevant to them. A student can read a review and know if the person that wrote the review is the same major as them, which can help determine if the content of the review is relevant to the student.


A preview of the reviewer profile is on the review so that the user can easily click on the preview to read more about the reviewer.


The reviewer profile will show the person’s major, year, and other classes they took while still keeping their identities anonymous.

Q&A and Threads

When a student is researching for their classes, they can have specific questions about the class. We decided to make the SchoolProven more of an interactive community, where students can communicate with each other and help each other answer questions about the class.


For every review page, there is also a Q&A page, where students can post questions about the class and other students can help answer them.



When looking at a review, there is an option to start a thread so that people can ask specific questions about the review itself and to the reviewer that wrote the review.

A/B Testing

When we performed user testing on our functional prototype, we found that many users spent little time digesting the information that was presented to them, whether it was reading the review, writing the review, or looking at a reviewer profile. In order to motivate users to spend more time on reviews, we decided to include the option to give endorsements to reviewers when a user opens up a profile. During the week that we ran the A/B test, we had a total of 39 users on our app. We set up our test to have 50/50 distribution of variants so that 50% of users would see the original design and the 50% of users would see the experimental design.

Results showed that the average duration per session increased in the experimental design over the original design, and so we decided to move forward with adding endorsements to our app.
